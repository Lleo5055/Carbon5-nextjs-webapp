# UK ETS Explained: What Businesses Need to Know

The UK Emissions Trading Scheme (UK ETS) is the government's primary market-based mechanism for reducing greenhouse gas emissions from energy-intensive industries, power generation, and aviation. Launched in January 2021 following the UK's departure from the EU ETS, it places a price on carbon and creates a financial incentive to reduce emissions over time.

This guide explains how the UK ETS works, which sectors and businesses are covered, how allowances are allocated, and what compliance looks like in practice.

## How the UK ETS Works

The UK ETS operates on a cap-and-trade principle. The government sets a total cap on the volume of greenhouse gas emissions permitted across all covered sectors. This cap is expressed in UK Allowances (UKAs), where one UKA represents one tonne of CO2 equivalent.

Covered businesses must surrender one UKA for every tonne of CO2e they emit each year. They receive a proportion of allowances for free (free allocation), purchase additional allowances at quarterly government auctions, or buy them on the secondary market from other participants.

The cap declines year on year, gradually tightening the overall limit and — in theory — increasing the carbon price over time as allowances become scarcer. This creates a structural incentive to invest in decarbonisation: the faster you reduce emissions, the fewer allowances you need to purchase.

### The Carbon Price Signal

UK ETS allowance prices have historically traded between £30 and £60 per tonne of CO2e, though prices fluctuate with energy markets, economic conditions, and government policy signals. A business emitting 100,000 tonnes of CO2e per year and purchasing all its allowances at the market price of £40/tonne faces a direct carbon cost of £4 million annually — a material incentive to invest in efficiency measures.

## Which Sectors Are Covered by UK ETS?

The UK ETS currently covers four main sectors:

**Energy and power generation** — Combustion installations above 20 MW thermal input, including power stations, industrial boilers, and combined heat and power (CHP) plants.

**Energy-intensive industries** — Including steel, cement, glass, ceramics, paper, and refining. These sectors receive substantial free allocation to protect against carbon leakage — the risk that production moves to countries without a carbon price.

**Aviation** — Commercial flights departing from UK airports to destinations within the UK and European Economic Area. Operators must surrender allowances based on verified CO2 emissions from covered flights.

**Offshore oil and gas** — Offshore combustion installations above 20 MW, added to the UK ETS from 2024 as part of the government's broader decarbonisation of the North Sea.

Businesses outside these sectors — the majority of UK SMEs, retail businesses, professional services firms, and most hospitality operators — are not subject to UK ETS. Their carbon obligations, if any, fall under the separate SECR reporting framework.

## UK ETS vs EU ETS: Key Differences

The UK ETS was designed to closely mirror the EU ETS at launch but has diverged in several important respects since 2021.

**Cap trajectory** — The UK ETS cap declines at a faster annual rate than the EU ETS, reflecting the UK's more ambitious near-term emissions reduction commitments.

**Linking status** — The UK ETS is not currently linked to the EU ETS, meaning UK and EU allowances are not interchangeable. This creates a separate UK carbon market with its own price. Discussions about linking have stalled, and UK businesses with operations in both jurisdictions must manage compliance in two separate systems.

**Aviation scope** — The UK ETS covers UK domestic and UK-EEA aviation. Flights between the UK and non-EEA countries are not currently covered. The EU ETS has a broader aviation scope under CORSIA alignment.

**Carbon border adjustment** — The EU is implementing a Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) from 2026, applying a carbon cost to imports from countries with weaker carbon pricing. The UK government has announced a UK CBAM for cement, aluminium, fertiliser, hydrogen, iron, and steel, also from 2027, affecting importers of these goods.

## Free Allocation and the Benchmark System

Not all UK ETS participants pay for every allowance. Free allocation is provided to energy-intensive sectors to prevent carbon leakage — the risk that manufacturers relocate production to countries without carbon pricing.

Free allocation is calculated using product benchmarks: the most efficient 10% of installations in a given sector set the benchmark for free allowances. If your installation is less efficient than the benchmark, you receive fewer free allowances than your actual emissions and must purchase the difference.

This design means the least efficient operators face the highest carbon costs, creating a direct financial reward for investing in efficiency improvements.

Free allocation is being progressively reduced for most sectors over the coming years, meaning more allowances will need to be purchased — and the carbon cost per tonne of production will rise for all covered operators.

## Compliance Obligations for UK ETS Participants

If your business operates within a covered sector, your annual compliance cycle looks like this:

**Monitoring and reporting** — You must implement an approved Monitoring Plan, measuring emissions from all covered sources at your installation throughout the year. The methodology must be approved by your regulator (the Environment Agency in England, SEPA in Scotland, Natural Resources Wales, or DAERA in Northern Ireland).

**Verification** — Annual emissions reports must be independently verified by an accredited third-party verifier before submission. Verification assures regulators that reported data is accurate, complete, and consistent with the Monitoring Plan.

**Surrender** — By 30 April each year, you must surrender UKAs equal to your verified emissions for the preceding year. Allowances surrendered are retired — they cannot be resold.

**Penalties** — Failure to surrender sufficient allowances by the deadline triggers a penalty of £100 per tonne of shortfall, plus the obligation to make up the deficit the following year. Persistent non-compliance can result in enforcement action.

## How Carbon Accounting Connects to UK ETS Compliance

[Carbon accounting in the UK](/blog/carbon-accounting-uk) covers the full landscape of measurement and reporting obligations. For UK ETS participants, carbon accounting is not optional — accurate measurement of covered emissions is legally required under the Monitoring Plan. The output feeds directly into the verified annual emissions report.

[What are carbon credits](/blog/what-are-carbon-credits) explains the broader voluntary carbon market and how it differs from compliance carbon markets like the UK ETS. A key distinction: UK ETS allowances (UKAs) are compliance instruments used to meet a legal obligation. Voluntary carbon credits (VERs) are used to offset emissions in voluntary sustainability commitments. They are not interchangeable.

## Does UK ETS Apply to Small Businesses?

No. The UK ETS applies only to large industrial installations and aircraft operators in covered sectors. A manufacturing plant below 20 MW thermal input is not covered. Professional services firms, retailers, restaurants, and most SMEs are entirely outside UK ETS scope.

For businesses outside the UK ETS, the relevant carbon reporting framework is SECR. [What is SECR reporting](/blog/what-is-secr-reporting) explains who must report and what is required.

## What Happens If I Acquire a Covered Installation?

If you acquire a business or site with a UK ETS permit, you inherit the compliance obligations from the date of acquisition. You must notify your regulator, transfer the permit, and ensure the Monitoring Plan remains valid. Allowances already surrendered by the previous operator are credited to the compliance account for the year in question.

## UK ETS Reform: What's Coming

The government has signalled several reforms over the coming years:

- Further tightening of the annual cap to align with the UK's 2030 and 2035 carbon budget commitments
- Potential expansion to additional sectors, including waste incineration and maritime
- Progressive reduction of free allocation for energy-intensive industries
- Possible linking with the EU ETS — though no timeline has been confirmed

Businesses covered by UK ETS should monitor these changes closely, as tightening caps and reduced free allocation will increase compliance costs significantly by 2030.

## Conclusion

The UK ETS is the cornerstone of the UK's industrial decarbonisation policy. For covered businesses, it imposes a direct and growing financial cost on emissions, with a compliance cycle that requires robust monitoring, independent verification, and timely allowance surrender. Understanding your allocation, tracking your emissions accurately, and investing in efficiency ahead of cap tightening are the foundations of a sound UK ETS compliance strategy. For businesses outside covered sectors, SECR remains the primary reporting obligation — but the principle is the same: measure first, then reduce.
